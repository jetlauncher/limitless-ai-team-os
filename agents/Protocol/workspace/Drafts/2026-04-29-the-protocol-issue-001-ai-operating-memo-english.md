# Main Issue

## Opening insight

Most business owners are not losing the AI race because they lack tools.

They are losing because they do not have an operating system for AI to work inside the company.

Over the past year, I keep seeing the same pattern.

Companies start AI with one question:

“What is the best tool right now?”

It is not a bad question.

It is just too small.

If you start from tools, you may get one person on the team who becomes better at ChatGPT. But the company itself does not get faster.

A better question is:

**Which recurring workflow in the company, if AI could handle 80% of it every week, would help sales move faster, customers get answered sooner, or the team make better decisions?**

That is the starting point for The Protocol.

This is not an AI trend newsletter.

Not a list of new tools.

Not theory about the future.

It is an operating memo for business owners who want AI to become part of the real work of the company.

## The shift

In the first phase, most people used AI as an assistant:

- writing captions
- summarizing meetings
- translating text
- making images
- brainstorming ideas

All useful.

But still mostly personal productivity.

One person gets faster. The company does not change how it works.

The shift now is this:

The companies that actually benefit from AI will not stop at “who can prompt better?”

They will turn recurring work into workflows.

Not AI as helper.

AI as operator for parts of the business.

For example:

- a new lead comes in → AI summarizes context → sales calls back with a sharper brief
- a customer asks a repeated question → AI drafts the answer from the standard → the team approves and sends faster
- a meeting ends → AI extracts decisions, owners, and deadlines → action items do not disappear
- a content idea appears → AI turns it into posts, emails, and scripts → the team can produce immediately

That is the difference.

**AI as a tool makes one person faster.**

**AI as a workflow makes the team move in the same rhythm.**

Tools don’t win.

Systems do.

## What it means for business owners

For Thai companies, this matters more than most people think.

The real issue is not only technology.

The real issue often looks like this:

- important work lives inside LINE chats
- decisions sit in the head of the owner or a few senior people
- SOPs exist, but nobody opens them
- salespeople answer customers differently
- the team is too polite to ask the same question twice
- meetings end without a clear owner
- customer data is scattered across sales, admin, and operations

If you put AI into this environment without redesigning the work, AI becomes another toy.

The team tries it for two weeks.

Everyone gets excited.

Then the company goes back to the old way of working.

AI does not fail because the team is bad at technology.

It fails because nobody redesigned the work so AI can become part of the system.

For the owner, the goal is not “make everyone use AI.”

The goal is:

**Make important work repeatable at a higher quality without depending on heroes all the time.**

That is the operator’s way to think about AI.

Not the software seller’s way.

## The Monday AI Workflow Audit

Take 60–90 minutes.

Open a blank document.

Write down 10 recurring workflows in your company.

Look for work that happens every week. Work that uses the time of good people. Work that, when done poorly, affects sales, customers, or decision speed.

Examples:

- summarizing a lead before sales calls back
- turning meeting notes into tasks
- answering repeated customer questions
- creating weekly sales insight from a CRM or spreadsheet
- turning long-form content into social posts
- checking a proposal before sending it to a client
- onboarding a new employee
- summarizing customer feedback every week

Then choose one workflow.

Not the biggest one.

Choose the one closest to money.

Write a one-page AI workflow using this format:

```text
Work:
Who does it today:
Minutes per occurrence:
How often it happens:
Where the input lives:
What output is needed:
Where AI can handle 80%:
Where a human must approve:
How many cases to test:
What metric proves it worked:
```

Simple rules:

Do not automate before you understand the work.

Do not buy a tool before you see the workflow.

Do not scale before you test it 20 times.

Start small.

But stay close to money.

## Tool / workflow example

Imagine your sales team receives leads from Facebook, LINE OA, website forms, and referrals.

The problem is that each salesperson interprets the lead differently.

Some call quickly.

Some call late.

Some ask questions the customer already answered.

This is where AI can help immediately.

You do not need to build a large system.

Start with a Lead Brief Agent.

### Input

- customer name
- company or industry
- the customer’s message
- lead source
- budget or pain point, if available

### Prompt example

```text
You are our sales assistant.
Your job is to read a lead and prepare a short brief so sales can call back better.

Summarize in a concise format:
1. What the customer likely wants
2. Pain points visible from the message
3. Three follow-up questions sales should ask
4. The sales angle to use
5. Urgency level: high / medium / low, with reason
6. Cautions when speaking with this customer

Lead information:
[paste lead data here]
```

### Output

Sales no longer starts from a rushed reading of the raw message.

They get a two-minute brief that helps them begin the conversation with better context.

AI does not close the sale.

AI helps the human start from a better place.

### Human approval

Sales still decides how to speak to the customer.

AI prepares the field.

The human plays the game.

### Measurement

Test this with 20 leads.

Look at three things:

1. Did response time go down?
2. Did sales ask fewer repeated questions?
3. Did lead-to-appointment conversion improve?

This is an AI workflow small enough to start on Monday.

But close enough to revenue to matter.
