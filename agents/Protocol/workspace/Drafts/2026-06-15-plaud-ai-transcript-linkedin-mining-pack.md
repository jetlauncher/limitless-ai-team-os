# 20 LinkedIn Posts Mined from Plaud AI Transcripts

## Post 1 — ChatGPT user vs AI operator

Most people are still using AI like a smarter Google.

Ask a question.

Get an answer.

Copy it somewhere else.

Do the rest manually.

That is useful.

But it is not the real leverage.

The next level is becoming an AI operator.

An AI operator does not just ask better questions.

They design work.

They give context.

They define the workflow.

They set approval gates.

They turn a one-time prompt into a reusable system.

Example:

Old way:

“Summarize this meeting.”

Operator way:

“Every meeting transcript should become decisions, open questions, owners, deadlines, follow-up drafts, CRM notes, and next agenda.”

That is a different game.

One is a chat.

The other is an operating rhythm.

The business owners who win with AI will not be the ones who know the most tools.

They will be the ones who can see repeat work and turn it into systems.

## Post 2 — Context is fuel

AI does not know your business.

It knows the internet.

Big difference.

It does not know:

- Why your customers hesitate
- What your sales team hears every week
- Which objections matter
- What your brand should never say
- How your best operator thinks
- What “good enough” looks like in your company
- Where your team usually drops the ball

This is why many owners try AI and feel disappointed.

The model is smart.

But it is working like an outsider.

The fix is not always a better model.

Often, it is better context.

Your SOPs.

Your best examples.

Your sales calls.

Your meeting notes.

Your customer questions.

Your past decisions.

Your internal standards.

All of that should become AI-readable company context.

The companies that win will not just have access to AI.

They will have better company memory.

## Post 3 — Onboard AI like an employee

Most companies onboard AI worse than they onboard interns.

They give it almost no context.

No examples.

No standards.

No workflow.

No definition of done.

Then they complain that the output feels generic.

Of course it does.

Imagine hiring a smart employee and saying:

“Help with marketing.”

That person would fail too.

A good AI onboarding should answer:

- Who are our customers?
- What do we sell?
- What do we not sell?
- What is our tone?
- What does a great output look like?
- What should never be done without approval?
- What examples should it copy?
- What edge cases matter?

If you want AI to work like a teammate, train it like one.

The quality jump usually does not come from a magic prompt.

It comes from giving AI the same business context you would give a serious new hire.

## Post 4 — AI Native is not ChatGPT usage

Using ChatGPT does not make a company AI Native.

It makes the company a ChatGPT user.

There is nothing wrong with that.

But do not confuse tool usage with operating change.

AI Native means the company has redesigned how work moves.

Context is captured.

Workflows are mapped.

SOPs become AI skills.

Agents have access to the right tools.

Humans review the risky parts.

Memory is saved so the system gets better over time.

That is very different from everyone having a Plus subscription.

One gives individuals pockets of speed.

The other changes company rhythm.

The question for leaders is not:

“Are our people using AI?”

Better question:

“Which processes in our company are now designed for AI to execute, with humans controlling quality?”

That is where AI Native begins.

## Post 5 — A skill is an SOP for AI

A strong AI skill is just a good SOP made executable.

Not a fancy prompt.

Not a paragraph of clever wording.

A real skill tells AI:

- What the task is
- What inputs it needs
- What steps to follow
- What examples to match
- What mistakes to avoid
- When to stop and ask a human
- What the final output should look like

That is why companies without SOPs struggle with AI agents.

They want automation.

But the work is still tribal knowledge.

It lives in someone’s head.

It changes depending on who is doing it.

It has no clear standard.

AI does not fix that.

It exposes it.

Before building your next agent, ask:

“If I had to teach a new employee this task tomorrow, do we have a clear enough SOP?”

If not, start there.

The SOP is the product.

The agent is the execution layer.

## Post 6 — Technical CEO

A Technical CEO in 2026 does not need to be the best coder in the room.

That is the wrong definition.

A Technical CEO can translate business problems into clear specs.

They can explain:

- Who the user is
- What pain point matters
- What data goes in
- What output comes out
- What workflow happens next
- What the edge cases are
- What “done” looks like

That skill is becoming more valuable because AI can now help with the build.

Mockups.

Databases.

Automations.

Internal tools.

Dashboards.

QA checklists.

Deployment steps.

But AI still needs a clear problem.

If the founder cannot define the workflow, the system will be fast but wrong.

The new bottleneck is not always engineering capacity.

Sometimes it is founder clarity.

The better you can describe the business problem, the more leverage AI gives you.

## Post 7 — Low-code does not remove thinking

Low-code and AI make building cheaper.

They do not make thinking optional.

This is the trap.

A founder sees a tool that can create an app quickly and thinks the hard part is gone.

It is not gone.

It moved.

The hard part is now:

- Choosing the right problem
- Defining the workflow
- Knowing the user
- Designing the data structure
- Setting the business logic
- Testing edge cases
- Protecting quality

AI can help you build faster.

But it will also help you build the wrong thing faster if your thinking is weak.

So before you open another no-code tool, write one page:

What problem are we solving?
Who uses this?
What happens before and after?
What does success look like?
What should never happen?

That one page may save you weeks.

Clarity is still the highest-leverage tool.

## Post 8 — Workflow before tool

Do not start with the AI tool.

Start with the workflow.

Most owners do it backwards.

They ask:

“Should we use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Manus, Codex, Zapier, n8n, Airtable, Notion, or something else?”

Wrong first question.

Ask:

“What work are we trying to move?”

Where does it start?

What information is needed?

Who decides?

What is the output?

Where does it go next?

What can AI do?

What must a human review?

Once that is clear, the tool choice becomes much easier.

Without that clarity, every tool looks interesting and none of them become infrastructure.

AI strategy does not begin with software.

It begins with the work.

Map the work first.

Then choose the tool.

## Post 9 — Human audit is the control layer

AI agents need freedom.

They also need control.

That sounds contradictory.

It is not.

A good AI workflow should let AI do the repeatable 80%.

Research.

Summarize.

Draft.

Compare.

Format.

Prepare.

Check.

But humans still own the parts that carry risk:

Judgment.

Taste.

Client trust.

Legal exposure.

Financial decisions.

Brand reputation.

Final approval.

The mistake is either extreme.

Some companies keep AI trapped in low-value tasks forever.

Others try to automate everything without review.

The better model is simple:

AI executes the workflow.

Humans audit the decision points.

That is how you get speed without losing responsibility.

AI should reduce manual work.

It should not remove ownership.

## Post 10 — Company Wikipedia

Every company needs a Company Wikipedia.

Not a pretty knowledge base that nobody opens.

A living brain that AI can actually use.

It should contain:

- Customer profiles
- Sales objections
- Offers
- Pricing logic
- SOPs
- Brand voice
- Approved examples
- Meeting decisions
- Product notes
- Team roles
- Past campaigns
- Mistakes to avoid

Why?

Because AI is only as useful as the context it can access.

If your company knowledge is scattered across WhatsApp, Google Drive, Notion, old decks, and people’s heads, your AI will stay shallow.

The goal is not documentation for documentation’s sake.

The goal is to make the business readable by machines.

Once the company brain exists, every AI agent becomes easier to train.

Every new team member becomes easier to onboard.

Every repeated decision becomes easier to improve.

Knowledge that stays scattered cannot scale.

## Post 11 — Founder knowledge must leave your head

If the founder’s judgment stays inside the founder’s head, AI cannot scale the company.

This is the uncomfortable truth.

Many businesses depend on invisible founder knowledge:

“This client is sensitive.”

“This type of lead is low quality.”

“We do not say it that way.”

“This offer needs a softer follow-up.”

“That design looks off-brand.”

“This decision creates a delivery problem later.”

The founder sees it instantly.

The team does not.

AI definitely does not.

Unless you capture it.

The next job of founders is not only making decisions.

It is turning their decision logic into company context.

Record the explanation.

Save examples.

Write the rule.

Turn repeated judgment into a checklist.

That is how AI starts to carry more of your operating mind.

Not perfectly.

But enough to remove you from the first 80% of repeated work.

## Post 12 — The first agent should not be ambitious

Your first AI agent should not try to run the company.

It should run one annoying workflow.

That is enough.

Pick something that is:

Repeated often.

Painful enough to matter.

Simple enough to define.

Low-risk enough to test.

Examples:

- Meeting transcript to follow-up notes
- Lead inquiry to qualification summary
- Competitor page to research brief
- Sales call to objection library
- Workshop transcript to content angles
- Customer feedback to product insight
- Weekly numbers to management memo

Do not begin with “build an AI COO.”

Begin with one workflow that saves two hours every week.

Then make it reliable.

Then turn it into a skill.

Then add the next workflow.

AI transformation is not one giant leap.

It is a series of small operating upgrades that compound.

## Post 13 — The 10-80-10 model

A useful way to think about AI work:

10-80-10.

The first 10% is human direction.

Define the goal.

Give context.

Explain the standard.

Set the constraints.

The middle 80% is AI execution.

Draft.

Research.

Summarize.

Compare.

Format.

Prepare options.

The final 10% is human review.

Check accuracy.

Apply judgment.

Make the decision.

Adjust taste.

Approve the final version.

Most teams fail because they skip the first 10%.

They give vague direction and expect great output.

Or they skip the final 10% and trust AI too much.

The leverage is in the middle 80%.

But the quality comes from the human 10% on both sides.

That is the operating model.

## Post 14 — Reusable work beats one-time prompts

A prompt that works once is useful.

A workflow that works every week is an asset.

This is the mindset shift.

Most people use AI for one-off tasks.

Write this email.

Summarize this call.

Make this caption.

Build this outline.

Then next week, they start again.

Better approach:

When a prompt works, ask:

“How do we turn this into a reusable system?”

Can it become a template?

A skill?

A checklist?

A scheduled task?

An agent workflow?

A Notion button?

An automation?

This is how AI stops being random productivity and becomes infrastructure.

The first win is getting AI to do the task.

The second win is making sure nobody has to reinvent that task again.

Do not just collect prompts.

Convert winners into operating assets.

## Post 16 — AI makes bad process visible

AI does not only speed up good work.

It reveals bad process.

Ask AI to automate a workflow and you quickly discover:

Nobody knows the exact steps.

The input changes every time.

The output standard is unclear.

The approval owner is vague.

The data lives in five places.

The exception rules are all in someone’s head.

This is not an AI failure.

It is a process mirror.

That mirror is useful.

Because once the mess is visible, you can fix it.

Define the input.

Write the steps.

Choose the owner.

Document the exceptions.

Create the review rule.

Then automate.

If AI breaks when touching a process, do not immediately blame the model.

Ask whether the process was ever clean enough to scale.

AI rewards operational clarity.

## Post 18 — Ask less from memory, more from system

If your team has to remember how everything works, your company is fragile.

This becomes obvious when you try to use AI.

AI cannot follow a process that only exists in someone’s memory.

It cannot match a standard nobody has written down.

It cannot respect a rule that was only mentioned once in a meeting.

It cannot learn from a decision that was never recorded.

The fix is not to make people remember more.

The fix is to make the system carry more.

SOPs.

Examples.

Checklists.

Decision logs.

Templates.

Company context.

AI skills.

When the system carries the memory, both people and AI perform better.

This is the real reason documentation matters now.

Not bureaucracy.

Leverage.

A documented company can train AI.

An undocumented company can only keep asking the founder.

## Post 19 — From meeting to machine

A meeting should not end as a transcript.

It should become a machine.

If the same discussion leads to the same type of work every week, automate the after-meeting flow.

Example:

Leadership meeting ends.

AI extracts decisions.

AI lists owners.

AI drafts follow-ups.

AI updates project notes.

AI flags risks.

AI prepares next agenda.

Human reviews.

System saves the decision.

Next week starts with context instead of confusion.

That is how you turn meetings from conversation into operating rhythm.

Most teams lose value after meetings because the insight stays trapped in notes.

The question is not:

“Did we record the meeting?”

The question is:

“What did the recording become?”

If the answer is only “a transcript,” there is more value to extract.

## Post 20 — AI transformation starts with one workflow

AI transformation sounds too big.

That is why many owners delay it.

They imagine a complete rebuild of the company.

New tools.

New team.

New systems.

New dashboards.

New everything.

That is not where I would start.

Start with one workflow.

One painful repeatable process.

One place where your team wastes time every week.

One output with a clear standard.

One owner who can review quality.

Map it.

Give AI the context.

Run it once.

Review.

Improve.

Save it as a reusable skill or automation.

Then do the next one.

This is how companies actually become AI Native.

Not by announcing a transformation project.

By upgrading how work gets done, one workflow at a time.
