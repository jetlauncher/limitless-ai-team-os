# Is AI Actually Worth It? 5 Months, Real Numbers, Honest Answer

**Target length:** 8–12 minutes

---

## Hook

The wrong question is: does AI cost too much?

That is the question I see people asking in every founder group, every Discord, every comment section.

“Is it worth the subscription?”

“Is it worth buying a new machine?”

And I get it. The numbers sound big.

A 100,000 baht Mac Studio.

A new M5 Max at 200,000 baht.

Monthly API costs on top of that.

But if you are comparing AI to software costs, I think you are doing the math wrong.

The right comparison is hiring.

And once you make that comparison, the math gets very different very fast.

I have hired somewhere between 100 and 200 people in my life. I have probably interviewed 500 to 1,000.

So when I tell you what AI replaced for me, I am not theorizing.

I am talking about something I have lived on both sides.

Five months ago I installed OpenClaw when everything blew up on Twitter.

I heard about it, I installed it, and I have been running it hard ever since.

This video is my honest answer:

Was it worth it?

What does it actually do for a business?

And how should you think about whether it is right for yours?

## Section 1: The Real Cost of Hiring

Let me be straight with you about people.

People are great.

I have worked with genuinely talented, loyal, hardworking humans who made my businesses better.

That is true.

Also true:

People are complicated.

And I say this as someone who is, by my own admission, an asshole sometimes.

We both bring our whole lives to work.

Your employee has a sick kid on Tuesday.

You have a bad week in November.

There is a personality clash that nobody wants to talk about.

Someone gets a better offer and leaves three weeks before a launch.

None of that is anyone’s fault.

It is just what humans do.

We have emotions. We have health issues. We have family crises that show up without warning.

Managing all of that is a real cost.

And most founders do not put it in the spreadsheet.

Think about what actually goes into hiring one person.

The job post.

The screening.

The interviews.

I have sat through somewhere between 500 and 1,000 of those over my career.

Each one is time.

Then the offer.

The onboarding.

The ramp period where they are not yet at full output.

Then the management.

The check-ins.

The feedback.

The performance conversations.

The miscommunications.

And sometimes, the exit.

That is before you talk about salary, social security, equipment, training, and all the hidden overhead.

Now compare that to an AI agent.

What does it cost?

Tokens.

A machine to run them on.

And your time to set the system up properly.

That is basically it.

There is no sick day conversation.

There is no two-week notice at the worst possible moment.

No team politics.

No motivation problem on a Monday morning.

If the agent stops working, you fix the prompt, improve the workflow, or refill the API credits.

The failure modes are much simpler.

I am not saying AI is better than people at everything.

I am saying the cost comparison is not:

“AI subscription versus no AI subscription.”

The real comparison is:

“AI agent versus everything that comes with a human hire.”

That is a very different conversation.

## Section 2: What AI Agents Actually Do Well

After five months of running OpenClaw, here is what I can tell you works.

First: repetitive, high-volume tasks.

Research.

Drafting.

Summarizing.

Processing information.

Turning messy inputs into structured outputs.

If you have work that follows a pattern, agents handle it well.

They do not complain.

They do not get bored.

They do not do it worse on Friday afternoon than they did on Monday morning.

Consistency is the thing people underestimate.

A good human does the task well most of the time.

An agent does it the same way every time, if the system is designed correctly.

Second: speed.

The output that used to take a day can take an hour.

The thing that used to take an hour can take ten minutes.

That compounds.

If your business runs on content, research, analysis, operations, or customer communication, that compression of time is direct leverage.

Third: availability.

There is no timezone.

There is no out-of-office.

You can run a process at midnight and it does not care.

Fourth: parallel work.

One person works on one thing at a time.

Agents can run multiple threads.

For a small team, that is the equivalent of adding headcount without adding the full complexity of headcount.

This is where AI starts to become interesting for founders.

Not because it writes cute captions.

Because it lets a small team operate like a bigger team.

## Section 3: What AI Agents Do Not Do

I do not want to oversell this.

Because overselling is how people end up disappointed.

Agents are not magic.

They need to be set up well.

If your input is vague, your output will be garbage.

The quality of what you get is directly tied to the quality of how you structure the task.

That setup work is real work.

It takes time.

It takes iteration.

You need to define the role.

You need to define the context.

You need to define what good output looks like.

You need to review the output and improve the system.

In that sense, AI is not replacing management.

It is changing what management means.

You are no longer just managing people.

You are managing systems.

Agents also do not replace judgment.

A senior person who has seen your industry for ten years brings pattern recognition that no model fully has.

Client relationships, strategic instincts, the read on a room, taste, timing, final calls.

Those stay human.

The point is not to remove humans from the business.

The point is to stop wasting human attention on work that machines can do well.

The agents free up time so your humans can spend more of it on judgment, relationships, and creativity.

And yes, agents break.

A process you set up in month one might need to be rebuilt in month three because the model changed, the tool changed, or your business changed.

That maintenance is not free.

Someone needs to own it.

If nobody owns the system, the system decays.

## Section 4: How to Evaluate If AI Is Worth It for Your Business

Here is the framework I would use.

### 1. Map your repetitive work

What tasks does your team do every week?

Research.

Writing.

Summarizing.

Data entry.

Customer response drafts.

Internal reporting.

Content repurposing.

Sales follow-ups.

Document cleanup.

List them.

Then estimate the hours per week.

That is your target.

Do not start with the most strategic thing in your business.

Start with the repeatable thing that is already costing you time.

### 2. Put a real cost on those hours

Do not just count salary.

Count the time you personally spend managing the person doing that work.

Count the overhead.

Count the errors you catch and fix.

Count the slowdowns when someone is out.

Count the meetings required to keep everything moving.

Most founders undercount this.

They look at salary and think that is the cost.

It is not.

The real cost is salary plus management drag plus inconsistency plus replacement risk.

### 3. Compare it to what AI setup actually costs

The machine.

The API credits.

The software.

The time to build and maintain the workflow.

For most founders, this number is smaller than they expect.

Not zero.

But smaller.

And more importantly, it scales differently.

A human hire adds fixed cost and management complexity.

An AI workflow adds usage cost and system complexity.

Those are not the same thing.

### 4. Run a test before you commit

Do not buy the 200,000 baht machine just to test whether AI is right for you.

Start smaller.

Pick one workflow.

Run it for 30 days.

Measure the output.

How much time did it save?

How many errors did it create?

How much review did it require?

Did it make the work faster, better, or just more complicated?

The machine is a scaling decision.

It is not the entry decision.

For me, the 100,000 baht Mac Studio was a compute decision once I knew the workflows were there.

The 200,000 baht M5 Max is the same category.

It is not “I bought a machine and hoped AI would work.”

It is “the workflows were already valuable, so more compute made sense.”

That distinction matters.

## Section 5: Is It Actually Making Money?

So the direct question:

Is AI making me money?

For me, yes.

And I can say that without giving you a specific revenue number, because the answer is not only about the revenue line.

It is also about what I am not spending.

I am not hiring as many people to do work that agents can now handle.

I am not managing the complexity that comes with those hires.

I am not absorbing the cost of turnover in the same way.

I am not losing as much time to operational drag.

My team can stay smaller and move faster.

The agents handle volume work.

The humans handle the work that requires judgment and relationships.

That is the actual ROI.

If you are a solo founder or a small team in Thailand or Southeast Asia, the leverage here is real.

You can operate at a level that used to require a much bigger team.

You do not need to be a tech company to use this.

You need to be willing to learn how to set it up properly.

That is the difference.

Most people ask:

“Which AI tool should I use?”

The better question is:

“Which part of my business is repetitive enough to turn into a system?”

If you can answer that, the tool choice becomes much easier.

## Section 6: The Honest Verdict

Five months in, here is where I land.

The hardware cost is real, but it is a one-time decision.

The API cost is ongoing, but it scales with usage, not headcount.

The setup cost is the part most people skip.

And that is why most people get mediocre results.

If you treat AI like a magic button, you will be disappointed.

If you treat it like a new category of team member that requires onboarding and management, you can build something that actually works.

I have hired and managed a lot of people.

AI agents are not people.

They are tools with a different failure mode and a much simpler cost structure.

For the right tasks, they are one of the best hires I have made.

For the wrong tasks, they are just an expensive distraction.

The job is figuring out which is which for your business.

And the only way to figure that out is to actually run the experiment.

Not read about it.

Not watch 50 videos.

Not argue about models on Twitter.

Run one workflow.

Measure the result.

Then decide.
