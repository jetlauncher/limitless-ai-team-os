### Draft 6 — AI ในบริษัทต้องมี Manager ไม่ใช่แค่ User

ถ้าเรามอง AI เป็นแค่ tool
เราจะสนใจว่าใครใช้มัน

เราต้องถามว่าใคร manage มัน

นี่คือช่องว่างใหญ่ในหลายบริษัท

มีผู้ใช้ AI จำนวนมาก
แต่ไม่มีเจ้าของระบบ AI ที่ชัดเจน

ไม่มีใครกำหนดว่า output แบบไหนผ่าน
ไม่มีใครดูว่า workflow ไหนควร automate
ไม่มีใครเช็กว่า AI คืนเวลาจริงไหม
ไม่มีใครเก็บ prompt ที่ดีเป็น process
ไม่มีใครตัดสินใจว่าเมื่อไหร่ต้อง escalate ให้คน

สุดท้าย AI จึงกลายเป็นนิสัยส่วนตัวของพนักงาน ไม่ใช่ capability ขององค์กร

ถ้าคุณอยากให้ AI เป็น operating layer คุณต้องสร้าง management layer ด้วย

อย่างน้อยควรมี 5 สิ่ง

1. Owner — ใครรับผิดชอบ workflow นี้
2. Standard — output แบบไหนถือว่าใช้ได้
3. Review — ใครตรวจ และตรวจอะไร

### 3 short reel scripts

#### Short Reel 1 — Stop asking which AI is smartest

Stop asking: “Which AI tool is the smartest?”

That is not the founder question.

The better question is:
“Which role in my company would create the most leverage if it got 60 minutes back every day?”

AI ROI does not come from intelligence in isolation.
It comes from capacity returned to the right job.

Give sales better follow-up.
Give managers earlier blocker detection.
Give finance faster anomaly checks.
Give founders cleaner decision briefs.

The winner is not the company with the most AI tools.
The winner is the company that knows exactly where AI gives time back.

CTA: Pick one role this week. Measure capacity, not usage.

#### Short Reel 2 — AI should not be a tool rollout

If your AI strategy is “give everyone access and hope productivity improves,” it is not a strategy.

It is a tool rollout.

A real AI strategy starts with one role, one workflow, and one metric.

What work should disappear from this person’s calendar?
Where should that time be reinvested?
What business result should move?

AI should not just create more output.
It should move human attention from low-value work to high-value decisions.

CTA: Do not measure prompts. Measure capacity.

#### Short Reel 3 — Role-specific AI beats general AI

General AI assistants feel impressive.
Role-specific AI assistants create business value.

A sales assistant that prepares follow-ups.
A manager assistant that surfaces blockers.
A finance assistant that flags anomalies.
A founder assistant that prepares decision briefs.

The narrower the job, the easier it is to measure.
The clearer the workflow, the faster the ROI.

AI does not need to do everything.
It needs to remove the right work from the right person.

CTA: Design AI like capacity for a role, not like another app.

### 3 stronger reel scripts with hook / beats / CTA

#### Stronger Reel 1 — The AI ROI question most founders are missing

Hook:
Most founders are asking the wrong AI question.

Beats:
They ask, “Which model is smartest?”
But the business question is, “Which role gets the most value if AI returns 60–90 minutes a day?”

Because AI ROI is not measured by how many prompts your team sends.
It is measured by what high-value work becomes possible again.

If sales gets time back, it should become more customer conversations.
If managers get time back, it should become faster blocker removal.
If finance gets time back, it should become cleaner decisions and earlier anomaly detection.
If founders get time back, it should become strategy, hiring, partnerships, and capital allocation.

The mistake is treating AI like a smarter app.
The advantage is treating AI like capacity added to a specific job.

CTA:
This week, choose one role. Write down the workflow, the time wasted, and the capacity you want back. That is where your AI strategy should begin.

#### Stronger Reel 2 — Usage is the wrong AI metric

Hook:
Your AI dashboard might be lying to you.

Beats:
If it only shows usage — seats activated, prompts sent, outputs created — it tells you activity.
It does not tell you business impact.

A team can use AI every day and still not move faster.
A company can generate more content, more notes, more drafts, and still not improve decisions.

The metric that matters is capacity.
Who got time back?
What work disappeared from the calendar?
Where was that attention reinvested?
Did the sales cycle shorten?
Did customer response time improve?
Did managers catch blockers earlier?
Did founders make better decisions with less context switching?

AI is only leverage if the returned time is pointed at higher-value work.
Otherwise, it is just another layer of activity.

CTA:
Stop celebrating AI usage. Start tracking capacity returned and where that capacity goes.

#### Stronger Reel 3 — Do not deploy AI to everyone equally

Hook:
One of the fastest ways to waste AI budget is to help everyone a little.

Beats:
That sounds fair, but businesses do not grow evenly.
They grow when bottlenecks are removed.

If sales is the bottleneck, AI should help lead research, call summaries, objection handling, and follow-up.
If support is the bottleneck, AI should help triage, answer drafts, escalation, and knowledge retrieval.
If management is the bottleneck, AI should surface blockers and prepare decisions.
If the founder is the bottleneck, AI should reduce noise and prepare decision briefs.

AI placed randomly creates scattered productivity.
AI placed at the bottleneck creates company-level speed.

So before buying another tool, map the workflow.
Where does work wait?
Who is overloaded?
Which repeated tasks eat the most valuable attention?

CTA:
Do not start with the tool list. Start with the constraint. Then put AI where leverage is highest.

### English LinkedIn post

Most companies are still asking the wrong AI question.

They ask:
“Which AI tool is the smartest?”

Founders should ask:
“Which role in our company would create the most leverage if it got 60–90 minutes back every day?”

That shift matters.

If you buy AI because it is smart, you get another tool.
If you design AI around a role, you get capacity.

A sales assistant that summarizes calls, prepares follow-ups, finds objections, and drafts proposals is not just “saving admin time.” It gives salespeople more time to sell.

A manager assistant that detects blockers, summarizes progress, and recommends next actions is not just “making reports faster.” It shortens the time problems sit unresolved.

A finance assistant that checks numbers, flags anomalies, and prepares cash-flow explanations is not just “speeding up spreadsheets.” It improves the speed and confidence of decisions near money.

This is where many AI rollouts fail.

They give everyone access, celebrate usage, and hope productivity improves.

But AI usage is not the same as business leverage.

The better operating question is:
- What repeated work is consuming this role’s attention?
- If AI returns an hour a day, where should that time be reinvested?
- What metric should improve: speed, quality, revenue, risk, response time, or decision quality?

AI should not be positioned internally as “a new tool to try.”

It should be designed as capacity added to a specific job.

The companies that win with AI will not be the ones with the longest tool list.
They will be the ones that know exactly where AI gives time back — and how that time turns into business advantage.

Measure capacity, not usage.
