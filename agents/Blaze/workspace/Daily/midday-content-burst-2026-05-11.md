## 2. English Reel Script
HOOK (0–3s)
If your AI can touch customers, money, or company reputation, it needs rails before it needs more prompts.

BEAT 1 (3–12s)
AI agents are no longer just answering questions. They are starting to do work: writing replies, preparing quotations, checking invoices, reviewing ad budgets, and updating systems.

BEAT 2 (12–25s)
That changes the founder question. It is no longer only: “Is this AI smart?” The better question is: “What is this AI allowed to touch?”

BEAT 3 (25–43s)
For every AI workflow, define four zones:
- Allowed: what AI can do immediately
- Draft only: what AI can prepare but a human must review
- Approval required: what needs a human before action
- Logged: where every action is recorded

BEAT 4 (43–58s)
A sales AI can draft a customer reply. But can it promise a refund? Can it approve a discount? Can it change a payment term? If the answer is unclear, you do not have an AI problem. You have an operating-system problem.

CTA (58–70s)
This week, choose one workflow — sales follow-up, customer support, invoice checks, or ad budget review — and write a one-page AI Safety SOP. AI should not be added to chaos. Use it to make the system clearer.

## 3. English X Post
If AI can touch customers, money, or reputation, it needs rails before it needs more prompts.

For every AI workflow, define:

1. Allowed — what AI can do immediately
2. Draft only — what AI can prepare for review
3. Approval required — what needs a human first
4. Logged — where every action is recorded

Example:
AI can draft a sales reply.
AI cannot promise a refund.
AI can prepare a quotation.
AI cannot approve a discount.
AI can review an ad dashboard.
AI cannot increase budget without approval.

When agents move from answering to doing, founders must design the operating rules.

This is not a technical detail.
It is how small teams use AI without losing control.

## 4. English LinkedIn Post
AI agents are moving from answering questions to doing work.

That is a big difference.

When AI only gives suggestions, the risk is mostly bad advice.
When AI starts taking action, the risk becomes operational:

- wrong customer promise
- wrong discount
- wrong refund
- wrong data access
- wrong budget change
- no record of what happened

This is why founders need to stop asking only:

“Which AI tool should we buy?”

A better question is:

“What is this AI allowed to touch?”

For every AI workflow, I would define four zones:

1. Allowed
What AI can do immediately without review.

2. Draft only
What AI can prepare, but a human must check.

3. Approval required
Anything involving money, customers, legal risk, brand trust, or sensitive data.

4. Logged
Where every action, decision, and reason is recorded.

Example: sales follow-up.

AI can summarize a LINE conversation.
AI can draft the next reply.
AI can prepare a quotation.

But a human should approve discounts, refunds, unusual payment terms, or promises that affect delivery.

That is not slowing AI down.
That is making AI usable inside a real company.

The companies that win with AI will not be the ones that let agents do everything.
They will be the ones that design clear operating rules so AI can move fast without making the business fragile.

This week, choose one workflow and write a one-page AI Safety SOP:

- what AI can do
- what AI can only draft
- what needs approval
- where the log lives

Do not add AI to chaos.
Use AI as the reason to make the work clearer.
