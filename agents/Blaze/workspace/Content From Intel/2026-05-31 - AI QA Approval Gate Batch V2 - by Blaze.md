## 9. English LinkedIn post

I spent an afternoon rereading what my AI system had produced over the previous month.

Some of it was excellent.

Some of it was quietly wrong.

Summaries with numbers that were slightly off.

Replies that did not sound like us.

Content that looked useful but felt like it came from a different brand.

Nothing crashed.

No warning appeared.

The work just kept moving.

That is the real risk with AI inside a business. Not a dramatic failure. A quiet drift you only catch after the output has already touched a customer, a report, or the brand.

Most companies are still asking, “Which AI tool should we use?”

The better question is:

Can we test what the AI does before it goes live?

Can we put approval gates before it touches customers?

Can we see logs, cost, and failure points?

Can we stop the system before it sends something off-brand?

This is no longer just a technology question.

It is an operations question.

AI can draft, research, summarize, and prepare.

But taste and judgment still belong to the people who built the business.

If AI touches customers, content, reports, or decisions, it needs QA like any other team member.

Speed is useful.

Trust is more useful.
