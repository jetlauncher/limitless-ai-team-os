# Hermes Desktop App for Business Owners
## Turn your computer into an AI operating partner — no coding required

**Audience:** non-technical business owners  
**Outcome:** by the end, students can use Hermes Desktop to research, draft, analyze, automate, and safely delegate real business work.

**Image direction:** premium classroom scene: business owners around laptops, AI assistant interface glow, dark navy + gold, modern executive education style.

---

# The Big Promise
## Hermes is not “another chatbot”

Most AI tools answer questions. Hermes can **take actions**:

- Read and write files
- Search the web
- Use browser pages
- Analyze images
- Run calculations
- Create reusable workflows
- Schedule recurring tasks
- Remember useful context across sessions

**Plain-English mental model:** Hermes is an AI teammate that can use tools on your behalf.

**Image direction:** simple comparison diagram: chatbot = speech bubble; Hermes = speech bubble + toolbox + calendar + memory.

---

# Why Business Owners Should Care
## The leverage is not “AI content.” The leverage is operational speed.

Hermes helps you compress tasks like:

- Market research → summary + next actions
- Customer feedback → themes + fixes
- Sales notes → CRM-ready follow-up
- Meeting transcript → action plan
- Competitor audit → offer ideas
- Weekly KPI check → owner briefing
- SOP creation → repeatable process

**Key idea:** if you do it more than twice, Hermes can probably help systemize it.

**Image direction:** business owner at dashboard, arrows from messy inputs to clean decisions.

# Part 1 — AI Agent vs AI Chatbot
## The difference is tools + memory + workflow

A chatbot can say: “Here is a plan.”

An agent can help execute:

- Find files
- Inspect data
- Draft outputs
- Verify results
- Create a repeatable skill
- Schedule a follow-up

**Teaching line:** “Do not ask Hermes for ideas only. Ask it to produce a usable business artifact.”

# The Owner’s Operating Loop
## Use this loop for almost every business task

1. **Goal:** what decision/output do I need?
2. **Context:** what should Hermes know?
3. **Source:** where should Hermes look?
4. **Output:** what format should it produce?
5. **Verification:** how should we check it?
6. **Repeat:** should this become a skill or automation?

**Prompt formula:** “Use [source] to create [output] for [audience], verify by [check].”

**Image direction:** circular loop with six labeled steps.

# Good Prompt vs Weak Prompt
## Business owners need outcome prompts

Weak prompt:
> Give me marketing ideas.

Better prompt:
> Act as my growth strategist. I sell [offer] to [audience]. Use the website copy below and produce 10 campaign angles, ranked by speed-to-test, with one landing-page headline and one ad hook for each.

Best prompt:
> After drafting, critique the top 3 for risk, differentiation, and evidence needed.

**Image direction:** before/after prompt cards with “vague” vs “operator-ready.”

# Safety Rule: Human Approves External Consequences
## Hermes can act, so owners must set boundaries

Use Hermes freely for:

- Drafting
- Research
- Analysis
- Internal documents
- Calculations
- Local file organization

Pause before:

- Sending emails/messages
- Posting publicly
- Spending money
- Deleting files
- Changing live websites
- Touching customer data

**Class mantra:** “Draft automatically. Publish intentionally.”

**Image direction:** green/yellow/red traffic-light workflow.

# Memory: What Hermes Can Remember
## Useful for stable preferences, not temporary task lists

Hermes can remember durable facts such as:

- Preferred tone
- Brand rules
- Recurring file locations
- Business model assumptions
- Tools/accounts you use

Do **not** store:

- Passwords
- API keys
- Sensitive customer details
- Temporary project status

**Student exercise:** ask Hermes to remember one safe preference.

**Image direction:** secure memory vault with small labeled cards; no passwords symbol.

# Prompt Lab #1 — Research Brief
## Test current research + synthesis

Copy/paste:

> I run a business that sells [offer] to [audience]. Research the current market for [topic]. Create a one-page owner brief with: key trends, customer pain points, competitor patterns, risks, and 3 recommended actions. Cite sources or explain where the information came from. Keep it practical and non-technical.

**Success check:** can you make a decision after reading it?

**Image direction:** executive research memo with source tags.

---

# Prompt Lab #2 — Offer Audit
## Test business strategy thinking

> Act as a brutally practical offer strategist. Here is my offer: [paste offer]. My target customer is [audience]. Audit it for clarity, urgency, proof, differentiation, pricing risk, and objections. Then rewrite the offer in 3 versions: simple, premium, and high-urgency. Finish with 5 experiments I can run in 7 days.

**Success check:** does it produce experiments, not just advice?

**Image direction:** offer page under a magnifying glass with improvement checklist.

---

# Prompt Lab #3 — Sales Follow-Up
## Test turning notes into action

> Here are notes from a sales conversation: [paste notes]. Extract: buyer goals, pains, objections, budget/timing clues, decision process, and next best action. Draft a warm follow-up email in my voice. Then create 3 follow-up reminders if they do not respond.

**Success check:** would you send the email after light editing?

**Image direction:** sales notes transformed into polished email drafts.

---

# Prompt Lab #4 — SOP Builder
## Test process documentation

> I want to document this process: [describe process]. Turn it into a simple SOP for a new team member. Include: purpose, required inputs, steps, quality checklist, common mistakes, and when to ask for help. Then create a 5-minute training script.

**Success check:** could a junior person follow it?

**Image direction:** SOP checklist with training timer icon.

---

# Prompt Lab #5 — Customer Feedback Themes
## Test text analysis

> Analyze this customer feedback: [paste reviews/messages/survey answers]. Group it into themes, count rough frequency, quote representative examples, identify urgent fixes, and suggest 5 product/marketing changes. Separate facts from interpretation.

**Success check:** does it reveal what to fix next?

**Image direction:** customer messages clustered into themes.

---

# Prompt Lab #6 — Hiring Scorecard
## Test management support

> I am hiring for [role]. Create a hiring scorecard for a non-technical business owner. Include outcomes for the role, must-have skills, interview questions, red flags, trial task, and a simple 1–5 scoring rubric.

**Success check:** can you use it in an interview this week?

**Image direction:** candidate scorecard dashboard.

---

# Prompt Lab #7 — KPI Explainer
## Test numbers + decisions

> Here are my numbers: [paste metrics]. Use a calculation tool if needed. Explain what changed, what likely caused it, what I should investigate, and what actions I should take this week. Show assumptions and formulas clearly.

**Success check:** does it separate metric movement from business meaning?

**Image direction:** KPI dashboard with “what happened / why / next action” columns.

---

# Prompt Lab #8 — Landing Page Critique
## Test screenshot or URL feedback

> Analyze this landing page [paste URL or attach screenshot]. Review it for above-the-fold clarity, target customer fit, proof, friction, CTA strength, mobile readability, and trust. Give me the top 10 fixes ranked by expected impact and ease.

**Success check:** are the fixes specific enough to implement?

**Image direction:** landing page annotated with numbered fix callouts.

---

# Prompt Lab #9 — Content Repurpose
## Test content leverage

> Turn this source material into business content: [paste transcript/notes]. Produce: 1 LinkedIn post, 1 email newsletter, 1 carousel outline, 5 short video hooks, and 10 tweet-style insights. Keep the ideas practical for [audience] and preserve my point of view.

**Success check:** does it sound like you, not generic AI?

**Image direction:** source transcript splitting into five content formats.

---

# Prompt Lab #10 — Automation Designer
## Test recurring-work thinking

> Help me design a safe Hermes automation for [recurring task]. Ask first: what data source, frequency, output format, delivery channel, and approval rule are needed. Then propose the automation prompt, safety boundaries, and how I should verify the first run.

**Success check:** does it include approval rules and verification?

**Image direction:** automation blueprint with data source → Hermes → report → owner approval.
