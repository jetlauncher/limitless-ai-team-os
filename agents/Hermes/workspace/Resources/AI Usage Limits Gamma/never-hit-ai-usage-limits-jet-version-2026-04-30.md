# Never Hit AI Usage Limits Again: The Founder Workflow for Getting 3x More Out of Claude, ChatGPT, and Your AI Team

Date: 2026-04-30  
Prepared for: Jedi Trinupab / Limitless Club  
Prepared by: Kelly / Hermes  
Source inspiration: Miles Deutscher’s X article on avoiding Claude usage limits. This is Jet’s own version, reframed for nontechnical founders building an AI Team OS.

---

## The real problem is not the limit. It is the workflow.

Most people think they hit Claude or ChatGPT limits because they are “using AI too much.”

That is usually not true.

They hit limits because they are using expensive AI in the most inefficient way possible:

- brainstorming inside the strongest model
- keeping one giant chat alive forever
- asking the model to remember everything manually
- making the AI rebuild the same output multiple times
- using one tool for every job
- letting the model think deeply when a simple answer is enough

The result is predictable: you run out of messages, lose momentum, and start treating AI like a scarce resource instead of a team.

The founder who wins is not the one with the most expensive subscription.

The founder who wins is the one with the cleanest operating system.

---

## Principle 1: Plan cheap, execute expensive

The biggest token leak is not typing. It is rework.

When you ask Claude or ChatGPT to build something before you know exactly what you want, you force the model to guess. Then you correct it. Then it rebuilds. Then you correct it again.

That is how one task becomes three tasks.

A better workflow:

1. Use a cheap or fast model for messy thinking.
2. Turn the idea into a clear brief.
3. Only then use the strongest model to execute.

For example, if you want to create a sales page, do not start by saying:

> “Build me a sales page for my course.”

Start with:

> “Interview me about the course, audience, offer, proof, objections, and desired tone. Then turn my answers into a one-page creative brief.”

Once the brief is clear, give that brief to the stronger model.

This one habit can save more usage than any technical hack.

---

## Principle 2: Stop using one giant chat

Long chats feel convenient, but they become expensive and messy.

Every time you continue a long conversation, the model has to carry more context. Some of that context is useful. Most of it is old decisions, dead branches, and irrelevant experiments.

Long chats create three problems:

- more token usage
- slower answers
- worse output quality

Use projects, folders, and restart prompts instead.

For each recurring area, create a dedicated project:

- Content Engine
- Sales Page Builder
- AI Course Curriculum
- Customer Research
- CEO Briefing
- Ads and Creatives
- Finance and Revenue

Inside each project, use fresh chats for fresh tasks.

A simple restart prompt:

> “Summarize this conversation into a reusable handoff prompt. Include my goal, decisions made, constraints, tone, open questions, and the next exact action. Make it concise enough to paste into a new chat.”

Three clean chats beat one bloated chat.

## Principle 3: Give your AI memory outside the chat

If your AI forgets who you are, it is not always the model’s fault.

Most users keep important context trapped inside random conversations.

That means every new task starts with the same wasteful ritual:

- re-explain your business
- re-explain your audience
- re-explain your tone
- re-explain your offer
- re-explain what you hate
- re-explain what “good” looks like

The solution is to create external memory.

At minimum, every founder should have three markdown files:

### 1. Founder Profile

Who you are, what you are building, your audience, your positioning, your voice, your preferences.

### 2. Business Context

Products, offers, pricing, target customers, proof, objections, funnel, current goals.

### 3. AI Operating Rules

How the AI should work with you: format, tone, approval gates, what to avoid, how to ask questions, when to summarize, when to start a new chat.

This is the difference between “AI as chatbot” and “AI as team member.”

A chatbot waits for context.

A team member already knows the operating system.

## Principle 4: Escalate models like a manager

Do not use your strongest model for everything.

A real company does not send the CEO to do every task. You do not need Opus, GPT-5.5, or the most expensive model to summarize notes, clean formatting, brainstorm hooks, or scrape basic research.

Use an escalation ladder:

### Level 1: Cheap / fast models

Use for:

- brainstorming
- summarizing
- formatting
- first drafts
- simple research
- checklists

### Level 2: Strong general models

Use for:

- writing usable drafts
- analyzing business context
- building lesson plans
- creating strategy options
- reviewing customer data

### Level 3: Best model

Use for:

- final strategy
- high-stakes writing
- complex reasoning
- code architecture
- investor-level decks
- decisions with real cost

The question is not “what is the best model?”

The question is “what is the cheapest model that can do this step well?”

---

## Principle 5: Split tools by job

Do not make one AI tool do everything.

Each tool has a different cost structure, strength, and usage limit.

Use the right tool for the job:

- Claude Code / Codex / Bolt for building
- Gamma for presentations
- Notion or Obsidian for memory
- Perplexity or web agents for research
- ChatGPT / Claude for reasoning and writing
- Local models like Qwen for private drafts and low-cost analysis
- Canva / image tools for design assets

If you burn Claude Code tokens making slides, that is bad allocation.

If you burn GPT-5.5 doing simple data cleanup, that is bad allocation.

A founder should think like an operator: route the task to the right specialist.

---

## Principle 6: Use low-effort mode by default

Most tasks do not need deep reasoning.

If you ask for:

- “summarize this”
- “turn this into bullets”
- “clean this copy”
- “make this shorter”
- “extract action items”

You do not need maximum thinking.

Use concise mode. Use low effort. Ask for shorter answers. Tell the AI when you are optimizing for usage.

A useful instruction:

> “Be concise. I am optimizing for low token usage. If this chat becomes inefficient, tell me to start a new one and give me a restart prompt.”

This turns the AI into a usage coach.

---

## Principle 7: Build reusable skills, not repeated prompts

If you ask the same prompt more than three times, it should become a system.

Examples:

- daily briefing
- content research sweep
- sales page audit
- X post generation
- customer insight analysis
- revenue summary
- lesson plan creation
- ad creative review
- founder shutdown review

Every repeated workflow should become one of these:

- a saved prompt
- a project instruction
- a markdown template
- a custom GPT / Claude project
- a Hermes skill
- an automation

The goal is simple: never pay tokens to reinvent the same workflow twice.

## The AI Team OS version

The real unlock is not “how to avoid Claude limits.”

The real unlock is building an AI Team OS.

That means:

- one place for memory
- one place for tasks
- clear agent roles
- approval gates
- model routing
- reusable workflows
- daily operating rhythm

In my setup:

- Kelly is chief of staff
- Signal handles research
- Blaze handles content
- Kaijeaw handles Thai content
- Zegna handles taste and curation
- Bolt handles apps and sites
- Qwen handles local/private work
- Protocol handles newsletter operations

This matters because when every AI has a role, the work gets routed properly.

You stop asking one chat to be your strategist, designer, coder, researcher, copywriter, memory, and project manager all at once.

That is how you save usage.

That is also how you get better work.

## A simple workflow you can copy today

Before any AI task, ask yourself five questions:

1. What outcome do I want?
2. What context does the AI need?
3. Which model/tool is cheapest but good enough for this step?
4. Is this a fresh chat or a project/task chat?
5. Should this become a reusable template?

Then follow this sequence:

### Step 1: Brainstorm cheap

Use a cheaper model or local agent to explore messy ideas.

### Step 2: Create the brief

Turn the messy idea into a structured brief.

### Step 3: Execute strong

Use the best model only when the brief is ready.

### Step 4: Save memory

Extract durable preferences, rules, and decisions into a memory file.

### Step 5: Make it reusable

If the workflow will happen again, save it as a template or automation.

---

## Final takeaway

Usage limits are not just a pricing problem.

They are a workflow mirror.

If your AI workflow is messy, limits show up fast.

If your AI workflow is clean, you get more output, better quality, and fewer interruptions — even on the same plan.

The future belongs to founders who stop treating AI like a magic textbox and start treating it like an operating system.

Plan cheap. Execute expensive. Keep clean memory. Split tools by role. Save repeated workflows.

That is how you stop hitting limits.

More importantly, that is how you build leverage.
