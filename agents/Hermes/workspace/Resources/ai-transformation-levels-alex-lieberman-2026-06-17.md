# Enterprise AI Rollout: 14-Level Progression

**Source:** Alex Lieberman / @businessbarista on X  
**URL:** https://x.com/businessbarista/status/2067271422016094349  
**Posted:** 2026-06-17  
**Extracted by:** Kelly / Hermes  

## Original Guide

Alex Lieberman says this is the most common level-by-level progression he sees after working with executives on AI rollouts for 14 months.

> Note: these levels can appear in different order or happen simultaneously rather than sequentially depending on the company’s context.

## The 14 Levels

### Level 1 — Company-wide AI audit
Run a company-wide AI audit, including:
- Mapping key processes
- Interviewing SLT / ELT
- Surveying rank-and-file employees

### Level 2 — Post-diagnostic readout
Finalize the diagnostic readout, laying out an AI transformation timeline by initiative.

Prioritize initiatives by:
- ROI
- Risk profile
- Cultural support

### Level 3 — Company Brain / data readiness
The company realizes its data ducks are not in a row.

This leads to work on the “Company Brain” panacea — centralizing, cleaning, structuring, and making company knowledge usable by AI systems.

### Level 4 — Coding agents for engineering/product
In parallel, the company starts investing in coding agents for the engineering and product organization.

### Level 5 — Enterprise LLM access for non-technical champions
The company gets enterprise access to a general-purpose LLM for a subset of non-technical AI champions.

### Level 6 — Enterprise LLM access for all non-technical employees
Enterprise access expands from champions to all non-technical employees.

### Level 7 — Workshops for ELT and AI champions
Run a small cohort of workshops for ELT and AI champions so they can get more value from the technology.

### Level 8 — Company-wide enablement
Roll out a training and enablement program company-wide.

### Level 9 — AI hackathon
The company runs an AI hackathon.

Purpose:
- Let employees surface solutions to problems found in the AI audit
- Discover new problems and use cases from the front lines

### Level 10 — Leadership prioritizes hacks
Leadership reviews and prioritizes employee hacks.

They decide which initiatives should move from prototype to production.

### Level 11 — First production AI build: quick back-office win
The first AI build is usually a quick win tied to a back-office process.

Characteristics:
- Clear attributable hard ROI
- Low expected cultural pushback
- Easier to measure and justify

### Level 12 — Token efficiency and cost optimization
Token efficiency and AI cost optimization become a major focus as the AI budget starts ballooning, especially in the engineering organization.

### Level 13 — Repeatable AI operating loop
As internal momentum builds and leadership gives AI a longer ROI leash, the company follows a repeatable cycle:

1. Identify problem
2. Map process
3. Prototype
4. Test, harden, secure, and measure
5. Scale

This loop leverages:
- Initial AI audit readout
- Hackathon findings
- New problems discovered through rollout

### Level 14 — From deterministic workflow to self-guided agents
The company moves further along the spectrum from deterministic workflows toward self-guided agents as its AI muscle expands.

## Extracted Operating Model

The progression can be condensed into five operating phases:

| Phase | Levels | Focus |
|---|---:|---|
| Diagnose | 1–2 | Audit processes, interview leaders, survey employees, prioritize initiatives |
| Prepare foundations | 3–4 | Fix data readiness and introduce coding agents |
| Enable people | 5–8 | Give access to LLMs and train champions/company-wide users |
| Generate use cases | 9–10 | Hackathons, employee prototypes, leadership prioritization |
| Productionize and scale | 11–14 | Quick ROI wins, cost controls, repeatable build loop, agentic systems |

## Practical Takeaways

- Do not start with tools alone; start with process mapping and use-case discovery.
- Data readiness becomes a blocker earlier than most leaders expect.
- Coding agents are often the fastest technical starting point because engineering/product teams can adopt quickly.
- Non-technical rollout works best through champions first, then broader access.
- Hackathons are useful when connected back to the audit and production roadmap.
- The first production build should be low-risk, measurable, and culturally easy to accept.
- Cost/token governance should be designed before usage balloons.
- The mature pattern is not one-off experiments; it is a repeatable loop from problem discovery to hardened production systems.
- Agentic workflows come later, after the company has built process, data, governance, and operational muscle.

## Common Reply Themes Captured by xAI Search

- Data readiness / “Company Brain” is one of the biggest early blockers.
- Token costs and budget shock become real once engineering usage scales.
- Executive buy-in and change management may need to happen even before Level 1.
- Bottom-up use cases often matter as much as top-down audits.
- Buying tools without changing workflows is a common failure mode.
